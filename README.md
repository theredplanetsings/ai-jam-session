# AI Prompting for Academic, Professional, and Personal Development

---

## What This Is

These videos are a six-part, hands-on prompt engineering series. Each video is designed to help students build practical, transferable AI skills applied directly to the academic and professional work they are already doing.

Each video runs for a few minutes and students can easily rewind whenever necessary.

The series is built on **Bloom's Revised Taxonomy**, structuring each video to move students from foundational recall through to original creation within a single jam.

---

## Who This Is For

Any Davidson student regardless of major, year, or prior experience with AI tools. Videos are designed to be immediately useful — students should leave each one with prompts they can apply to their work that same week.

## Video Descriptions

### Video 1 — Writing & Essays
**Subtitle:** *Using AI as a Thinking Partner, Not a Ghostwriter*

**The core idea:** AI is most powerful in the writing process when it challenges you — not when it does the work for you. This video teaches students to use prompt engineering to stress-test arguments, get unstuck, and sharpen their writing while keeping their voice and reasoning at the center.

**Prompt engineering skills covered:**
- Role assignment — giving AI a specific perspective or persona to argue from
- Iteration — prompting in rounds, building on previous outputs
- Constraint-setting — using deliberate limits to produce more precise, energetic writing

**Three signature prompts:**
- **The Steelman** — ask AI to make the strongest possible case against your thesis, so you can strengthen your argument before your professor does
- **The Diagnostic** — paste a paragraph and ask AI to identify the weakest sentence, the vaguest claim, and the unanswered question — then you do the revision
- **The Options** — ask for three different versions of your introduction with different tones and structures, then choose and write your own

**Bloom's emphasis:** Apply → Evaluate → Create

**Video focus:** The distinction between using AI as a ghostwriter versus a sparring partner; the weak vs. strong prompt comparison; the step-by-step writing workflow that keeps the student's reasoning central throughout.

---

### Video 2 — Research & Reading
**Subtitle:** *Navigating Sources, Summaries, and the Literature Landscape*

**The core idea:** AI is only as useful as the purpose you give it. This video teaches students to lead every research prompt with their specific paper argument and reading goal — transforming generic summaries into targeted, strategically useful research support.

**Prompt engineering skills covered:**
- Context-setting — leading every prompt with purpose, paper topic, and what you need from the source
- Scaffolding prompts — building comprehension before reading, not just after
- Question generation — surfacing what you don't know yet to sharpen your research agenda

**Three signature prompts:**
- **The Purpose-Driven Summary** — frame your thesis first, then ask AI to summarize only what's relevant — including what complicates your argument
- **The Gap Finder** — after any summary, ask what's missing, what a skeptic would say, and where your paper fits in the conversation
- **The Pre-Reading Primer** — before diving into unfamiliar territory, ask AI for key terms, major debates, important thinkers, and current scholarly questions

**Bloom's emphasis:** Understand → Analyze → Create

**Video focus:** Why purpose-framing transforms research prompts; the gap-finding sequence as the foundation of a literature review; a caution against trusting AI summaries without verifying against the original source.

---

### Video 3 — Studying & Test Prep
**Subtitle:** *Building Custom Study Tools with Prompt Engineering*

**The core idea:** Generic study tools produce generic preparation. This video teaches students to feed AI their actual lecture notes, syllabus language, and course material — generating personalized quizzes, targeted explanations, and simulated exam pressure that maps directly to their specific course.

**Prompt engineering skills covered:**
- Specificity — feeding AI real course material rather than abstract topic names
- Format control — directing AI on question types, difficulty levels, and output structure
- Role-play prompting — putting AI in the role of professor, tutor, or examiner

**Three signature prompts:**
- **The Custom Quiz** — paste actual lecture notes and ask for a mixed-format quiz including application questions, with answers and explanations — taken before looking at the answers
- **The Concept Translator** — ask for the same concept explained three ways: plain-language analogy, formal definition, and exam-answer-key precision — then explain it back without looking
- **The Professor Simulation** — ask AI to quiz you one question at a time, critique your answers, and ask follow-ups, then deliver a gap analysis at the end

**Bloom's emphasis:** Remember → Understand → Analyze

**Video focus:** The passive review trap and why it fails; active recall as the most evidence-backed study method; the "close the screen and explain it yourself" test as the benchmark for real understanding.

---

### Video 4 — Data & Quantitative Work
**Subtitle:** *Thinking Through Numbers with AI as Your Step-by-Step Partner*

**The core idea:** AI is not a calculator — it is an exceptionally patient quantitative tutor. This video teaches students to use AI to build genuine understanding of methods, interpret results in context, and audit their own reasoning — not to execute calculations on their behalf.

**Prompt engineering skills covered:**
- Step-by-step prompting — asking AI to explain the logic behind each operation, not just the operation itself
- Context-setting — describing your dataset, research question, and course level before asking anything
- Error-checking prompts — using AI to find the holes in your reasoning before submission

**Three signature prompts:**
- **The Concept Trio** — ask for the same concept explained intuitively with an analogy, formally like a textbook, and practically in terms of what it means in real data — then explain it back without looking
- **The Step-by-Step Walkthrough** — walk through a real problem asking AI to explain why each step is necessary and what would go wrong if skipped
- **The Logic Audit** — write out your reasoning in plain English and ask AI to find errors, overlooked assumptions, or method mismatches before you submit

**Bloom's emphasis:** Understand → Analyze → Evaluate

**Video focus:** The distinction between executing a procedure and understanding it; a clear statement that AI makes math errors and outputs must be verified; the academic integrity argument framed around long-term skill-building rather than rule-following.

### Video 5 — Creative & Interdisciplinary Projects
**Subtitle:** *Prompting for Originality, Style, and Big Swings*

**The core idea:** Specificity is the engine of creative prompting — the more precise the constraint, the more original the output. This video teaches students to use AI to expand their creative range, generate unexpected angles, and push their work across disciplinary boundaries, while keeping their own voice and judgment in charge of every final decision.

**Prompt engineering skills covered:**
- Tone and style control — specifying register, aesthetic, and creative mode precisely
- Constraint-based prompting — using deliberate creative limitations to unlock more original territory
- Audience prompting — asking AI to respond as specific imagined readers to surface blind spots

**Three signature prompts:**
- **The Constraint Generator** — ask AI for six creative constraints of different types (structural, perspectival, tonal, disciplinary), pick the most interesting one, and work with it for ten minutes
- **The Pitch Shapeshifter** — pitch your project as a TED talk, a tweet, a grant proposal, and a movie logline — and identify which version reveals what your project is really about
- **The Discipline Mashup** — apply two unexpected disciplinary lenses to your project and harvest the questions each field would ask that your own field typically doesn't

**Bloom's emphasis:** Apply → Evaluate → Create

**Video focus:** Why generic prompts produce generic creative output; the counterintuitive argument that constraints produce more originality than open prompts; the distinction between AI's fluency and the student's irreplaceable point of view.

---

### Video 6 — Career & Personal Development
**Subtitle:** *Prompting for Clarity, Confidence, and Next Steps*

**The core idea:** Career materials and professional clarity require self-knowledge first and polished documents second. This video teaches students to give AI real context about who they are — not generic descriptions — and to use it as a thinking partner for both document refinement and deeper career reflection, while recognizing what AI cannot do: replace the perspective of someone who knows their context, campus, and field.

**Prompt engineering skills covered:**
- Persona prompting — putting AI in the role of recruiter, hiring manager, or demanding interviewer
- Feedback loop prompting — using AI to critique and iterate in rounds rather than generate once
- Specificity — giving AI real background, real goals, and real anxieties for maximally relevant output

**Three signature prompts:**
- **The Resume Story Audit** — ask AI what story your resume is actually telling and what a recruiter in your target field would think after thirty seconds — then ask what changes would shift that story
- **The Hard Interview** — ask AI to act as a demanding interviewer and ask the five hardest questions for your specific background and target role, one at a time, with critique after each answer
- **The Decision Mapper** — describe a career uncertainty and ask AI to ask you five clarifying questions before offering any recommendation, then ask what your answers reveal about what you actually want

**Bloom's emphasis:** Analyze → Evaluate → Create

**Video focus:** Why generic career prompts produce generic career materials; the importance of rewriting AI output in your own voice; a genuine acknowledgment that AI does not know your campus, your professors, or your specific context — and that career advisors, mentors, and alumni remain irreplaceable for that reason.

## Content

- The core problem the video addresses, grounded in a relatable student scenario
- The big idea — the conceptual shift the video is built around
- Three named, reusable prompts with weak vs. strong examples
- A clear "what not to do" section addressing misuse, shortcuts, and limitations
- A step-by-step workflow showing how the prompts fit into a real academic task
- A call to action connecting the video to the in-person jam video

Videos are intentionally conversational and peer-to-peer in tone — not lectures, not tutorials. They are designed to feel like a knowledgeable classmate walking you through something useful.

---

## What This Series Is Not

- It is not a tutorial on how to get AI to do your work for you
- It is not a series of productivity hacks or shortcuts
- It is not a replacement for reading, writing, thinking, or engaging with your professors and peers
- It is not discipline-specific — every video is designed for any Davidson student regardless of major

---

## A Note on Academic Integrity

Every video in this series is designed around a core principle: AI is a tool for thinking, NOT a substitute for it. The prompts taught across all six videos are oriented toward using AI to challenge, clarify, critique, and expand student thinking — not to produce work that students then submit as their own.

Students are always encouraged to check their institution's academic integrity policies and individual course guidelines regarding AI use. When in doubt, ask your professor directly.
